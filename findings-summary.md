# Colchester, CT Budget Analysis — Key Findings

## Executive Summary

This analysis examines 18 years of adopted budget data for the Town of Colchester, Connecticut (FY 2007-08 through FY 2024-25). The overarching finding is that Colchester has been fiscally restrained: total budget growth (+33.5%) has significantly lagged cumulative inflation (+51.3%), and real per-capita spending has declined 13.1% over the period.

---

## Section 1: Overall Budget Trends

### Finding 1.1 — Budget Growth Has Lagged Inflation
Colchester's total budget grew from $46.9M (FY 07-08) to $62.7M (FY 24-25), a nominal increase of 33.5%. Over the same period, cumulative CPI inflation was 51.3%. The budget has consistently tracked below inflation since FY 2009, meaning the town has delivered fewer real-dollar services each year relative to what inflation would suggest.

**Chart 1** (Budget Growth vs. Inflation, indexed) shows the gap widening steadily, reaching 17.8 percentage points by FY 24-25.

### Finding 1.2 — Annual Increases Are Modest
In 13 of 17 years measured, the annual budget increase was at or below the inflation rate. The compound annual growth rate (CAGR) is just 1.7%, compared to average CPI of 2.7%.

**Chart 2** (Annual Budget Change vs. CPI) visualizes this year by year.

### Finding 1.3 — Real Per-Capita Spending Has Declined
Adjusting for both inflation and population change, spending per resident fell from $3,029 (FY 07-08) to $2,631 (FY 24-25) in constant dollars — a 13.1% decrease.

**Charts 3 and 4** show the decline and the interplay between budget growth, population, and inflation.

### Finding 1.4 — Budget Composition Is Stable
The BOE consistently comprises about 71-73% of the total budget. The split between town government and BOE has remained remarkably stable across the entire 18-year period, with no evidence of either side crowding out the other.

**Chart 6** (stacked bar) illustrates this stability.

## Section 2: Town Government

### Finding 2.1 — Town Operating Budget Grew Even Slower Than BOE
The town operating budget grew just 25.4% over 18 years (from $13.6M to $17.1M), compared to 36.8% for the BOE. After adjusting for inflation, town government services have seen real cuts.

**Chart 5** (Town vs. BOE indexed) shows the town line consistently below both BOE and CPI.

### Finding 2.2 — Mill Rate Is Below Regional and Peer Averages
At 28.67 mills (FY 24-25), Colchester's mill rate is the 3rd lowest among its 7 neighboring towns and well below the peer average. Only Lebanon (21.00) and East Haddam (26.76) are lower among neighbors. Among demographic peers statewide, Colchester is also in the lower tier. However, mill rate comparisons must account for property revaluation timing — towns marked with an asterisk in the charts recently revalued.

**Charts 7 and 9** show regional and peer mill rate comparisons.

### Finding 2.3 — Per-Capita Budget Is Lowest Among Peers
At $3,979 per resident, Colchester spends less per person than every neighboring and demographic peer town compared. The peer average is $4,334 — about $355 more per resident than Colchester.

**Charts 8 and 10** show per-capita comparisons.

### Finding 2.4 — Town Insurance Costs Have Roughly Tracked Inflation
Town health insurance grew from $729K to $1.12M (+54.1%), close to cumulative CPI of 51.3%. Total town insurance (health + workers comp + property/liability + unemployment) grew from $1.25M to $2.02M (+61.5%). As a share of the town budget, insurance rose modestly from 7.4% to 9.9%. The detailed BOE-side insurance breakdown is not available in later budget PDFs (only FY 07-08 and 08-09 had itemized BOE benefits).

**Chart 15b** shows insurance trends with a CPI-adjusted baseline.

## Section 3: Board of Education

### Finding 3.1 — ECS Funding Has Been Effectively Cut
Colchester received $13.0M in Education Cost Sharing (ECS) grants from the state in FY 07-08. By FY 24-25, ECS had been reduced to $12.0M — a nominal 7.2% cut. Adjusted for inflation, ECS should be $19.2M to maintain the same purchasing power. The gap between actual and inflation-adjusted ECS is now $7.2M per year. This represents a cumulative loss of approximately $59M in state education funding over the 18-year period.

Colchester is classified as "overfunded" under the ECS formula and has been "held harmless" (frozen at $12.04M) since FY 2020-21. The FY 2010-11 dip to $11.6M was due to state budget cuts partially offset by federal ARRA funds.

**Chart 11** (ECS Actual vs. Inflation-Adjusted) shows the growing gap. **Chart 12** (ECS as % of BOE Budget) shows the declining contribution from 36.8% to 26.4%.

### Finding 3.2 — Teacher Staffing Has Declined With Enrollment
Total teachers (gen ed + sped) declined from 231.6 to 189.5 FTE (-18.2%), while enrollment fell from 3,267 to 2,235 (-31.6%). The teacher decline has been less steep than enrollment, meaning the student-teacher ratio has actually improved. Gen ed teachers specifically went from 205.1 to 161.5 FTE (-21.2%).

### Finding 3.3 — Administration Has Not Grown
School-level administrators (principals, APs, dept chairs) went from 10.0 to 9.0 FTE — a net decrease. Central office administrators declined from an estimated 4.0 to 2.8 FTE. No assistant superintendent position was found in any budget PDF across the entire 18-year period. Instructional specialists fluctuated between 11.6 and 16.7 FTE with no clear upward trend — the initial hypothesis that specialists had "tripled" was corrected when actual EdSight data was analyzed.

**Charts 13, 13b, and 14** show staffing composition and trends relative to enrollment decline.

### Finding 3.4 — Special Education Staffing Has Grown Moderately
Total sped staff grew from 76.5 to 93.0 FTE (+21.6%), driven primarily by sped paraprofessionals (50.0 → 65.0, +30.0%). Sped teachers stayed roughly flat (26.5 → 28.0, +5.7%). This growth occurred while overall enrollment declined 31.6%, likely reflecting increased identification of students needing services and expanded federal/state mandates.

**Chart 14b** compares sped staffing growth against gen ed teacher and enrollment declines.

### Finding 3.5 — Gen Ed Paraprofessional Spike Is ESSER-Funded and Temporary
The most dramatic staffing change was Gen Ed Paraprofessionals jumping from 33 to 89 FTE (+170%) in a single year (FY 24-25). This occurred at every school in the district and does not appear to be reclassification (Other Non-Instructional staff did not decline). The spike aligns with statewide and national trends driven by federal ESSER/ARPA COVID relief funding, with ESSER III obligation deadlines in September 2024. These positions are likely temporary — CT Mirror and NCTQ have reported that paraeducators are among the first positions cut as ESSER funds expire.

### Finding 3.6 — Salary Growth Has Lagged Inflation
Average teacher salary grew from ~$64,500 to ~$85,500 (+32.6%), while CPI grew 45.1% over the same period (FY 08-09 to FY 24-25). Colchester's average teacher salary of $82,554 (Niche.com verified) is below the CT statewide average of $86,511.

**Chart 15** compares actual vs. CPI-adjusted salary trajectories.

### Finding 3.7 — Per-Student Cost Has Risen Due to Enrollment Decline
BOE cost per student has risen from ~$11,100 to ~$20,400, largely because enrollment dropped 31.6% while the budget grew 36.8%. Education has significant fixed costs (facilities, administration, transportation routes) that don't scale down proportionally with enrollment. Even so, Colchester remains below the CT state median of $25,225 per student.

**Chart 16** shows per-student cost growth.

## Overall Conclusion

Colchester's budget has grown more slowly than inflation for 18 consecutive years. Real per-capita spending has fallen 13.1%. The town has the lowest per-capita budget among its peers, a below-average mill rate, below-average teacher salaries, and no evidence of administrative bloat. The BOE has managed declining enrollment by reducing teaching staff roughly proportionally, while special education staffing has grown modestly in response to mandated services. The most notable recent change — the Gen Ed Para spike — is attributable to temporary federal funding rather than local budget decisions. State ECS funding has been effectively cut by $7.2M/year in real terms, shifting more of the education funding burden to local property taxes. By any reasonable measure, Colchester has been fiscally restrained.
