ECS as a Percentage of the BOE Budget

The state's share of funding for Colchester schools has steadily eroded, shifting the burden to property taxes.

Key takeaway: The state covered 36.8% of the BOE budget in FY 2007-08. Today it covers just 26.4% — a decline of over 10 percentage points. Colchester is classified as "overfunded" under the ECS formula and has been held harmless (frozen) since FY 2020-21, but rising costs mean the real value erodes every year. The CT Conference of Municipalities estimates $820 million in missed inflationary adjustments statewide since 2013.

The Real Driver: Enrollment Decline, Not Hiring

Percentage change from FY 2008-09 baseline. If the district were "bloating staff," you'd see staffing growing while enrollment falls. Instead, everything declined — enrollment just declined the most.

Key takeaway: Enrollment plummeted 31.6% — far more than any staffing category. Teachers declined 18.2%, school-level admin declined 10%, and instructional specialists grew modestly. The district cut staff, just not as fast as students left — because you can't cut half a principal or close half a building. This is what drives the rising cost-per-student, not runaway hiring.

Actual dollar values for average teacher and administrator salaries. Dashed lines show where each would be if they had kept pace with CPI inflation.

Key takeaway: Administrator salaries have grown 37.9% over the period, outpacing teacher salary growth of 32.6%. The admin-to-teacher salary ratio has stayed relatively stable at around 1.47–1.53x.

BOE Cost per Student Over Time

Total BOE budget divided by student enrollment. Rising cost-per-student reflects enrollment decline more than spending growth.

Summary: Colchester's Spending is Under Control

Over the past 18 fiscal years, Colchester's total budget has grown from $46.9 million to $62.7 million — an increase of 33.5%. During that same period, cumulative inflation (as measured by the Consumer Price Index) rose 51.3%. This means the town's budget has grown at a rate significantly below inflation.

When you factor in population (which has remained essentially flat at around 15,500–16,000 residents), the picture becomes even clearer. Inflation-adjusted spending per resident has actually declined by 13.1% since FY 2007-08. Colchester residents are getting more government services for fewer real dollars per person.

The town's annual budget compound growth rate of just 1.7% reflects disciplined fiscal management — including years with flat or negative budgets (FY 2009-10, FY 2012-13, FY 2020-21). Even the largest single-year increase (5.1% in FY 2024-25) came after years of below-inflation growth and remains modest by any municipal standard.

Compared to its neighbors, Colchester's FY 2024-25 mill rate of 28.67 is the 3rd lowest among 7 surrounding towns and 8% below the neighbor average of 31.1 mills. East Hampton (38.04) and Marlborough (36.29) have significantly higher mill rates.

Among demographic peer towns statewide (similar population, income, and character), Colchester has the lowest per capita spending at $3,979 — 8.2% below the peer average of $4,334. Ellington ($4,295), East Hampton ($4,326), Ledyard ($4,328), and Tolland ($4,390) all spend more per resident. This confirms that Colchester is not just keeping pace — it's leading in fiscal efficiency.

Despite a dramatic 31.6% decline in student enrollment (3,267 to 2,235), the school system has maintained strong student-to-teacher ratios, improving from 14.1:1 to 11.8:1. Official CT EdSight FTE data — verified from state CSV reports for every year — shows teachers declined 18.2% (231.6 → 189.5 FTE), proportionally less than the enrollment drop, reflecting a commitment to manageable class sizes. School-level administrators (principals, APs, dept chairs) have been remarkably stable at 9–10 FTE throughout the entire 17-year period. Instructional specialists (coaches, curriculum coordinators) have fluctuated between 11–17 FTE with no sustained growth trend. Both teacher and administrator salary growth (32.6% and 37.9% respectively) has trailed inflation, meaning real compensation has declined in purchasing power terms. The BOE cost per student rose 83.4%, but this is driven primarily by enrollment decline and fixed costs that don't scale — Colchester's per-pupil spending of ~$20,400 remains below the state median of $25,225.

Perhaps most striking is the erosion of state education funding. The ECS (Education Cost Sharing) grant — the state's primary education aid — peaked at $13.8 million in FY 2013-14 and has been frozen at $12.04 million since FY 2020-21. If ECS had simply kept pace with inflation from FY 2007-08, Colchester would receive $19.2 million today instead of $12.0 million. Over 18 years, this has cost the school system a cumulative $42.2 million in lost purchasing power — all of which local property taxpayers have had to absorb. The state's share of the BOE budget has fallen from 36.8% to 26.4%, a decline of more than 10 percentage points.
